# Research Positioning

## Literature Landscape

Research on ESG disclosure assessment has historically relied on relatively coarse proxies such as checklist-based scoring, keyword counts, and broad corporate social performance indicators. These approaches are useful for building large-sample datasets, but they often treat disclosure as a surface-level text frequency problem rather than a semantic quality problem. In parallel, work in corporate disclosure has long shown that the clarity, ambiguity, and precision of narrative reports materially affect how information is interpreted by external stakeholders. For example, \citekey{LI2015} demonstrates that firms strategically adjust disclosure precision and readability in response to market incentives, highlighting that disclosure quality is not reducible to the presence or absence of ESG-related terms. This is especially relevant for ESG reporting, where meaningful assessment should distinguish substantive, specific, and decision-useful disclosures from boilerplate language.

A second strand of literature supports the need for context-sensitive evaluation tools. Studies in management and information systems emphasize that digital technologies can fundamentally reshape how organizations generate, process, and use information, but they also caution that digitization requires methods tailored to the task and context \citekey{Berger2021}. Related work on organizational capabilities and sustainability performance suggests that firms differ systematically in their ability to develop and communicate sustainability-related practices \citekey{Gelhard2016}. In the ESG disclosure setting, this implies that disclosure quality likely reflects not only compliance pressure but also internal capabilities, governance structures, and strategic orientation. Conventional rule-based scoring methods are too blunt to capture these nuanced differences, particularly when firms use narrative framing, mixed evidence, or domain-specific terminology.

The methodological literature in NLP and AI further motivates LLM-assisted assessment. Recent work shows that modern language models can support fine-grained document understanding tasks, including denoising, extraction, classification, and multilingual transfer. For instance, document-level extraction and evaluation remain challenging because information is dispersed across long texts and human-level performance is still difficult to match \citekey{Tong2022}. Cross-lingual and multilingual settings introduce additional instability and sensitivity to data design \citekey{Zhao2021}, which matters for Taiwan-listed companies reporting in Chinese or bilingual formats. At the same time, scholarship on large language models warns that evaluation quality, transparency, and dataset curation are critical to avoid unreliable or biased outputs \citekey{Bender2021}. This creates a strong rationale for a carefully validated, auditable LLM framework that can be benchmarked against expert judgments rather than assumed accurate because it is automated.

For the Taiwan context specifically, the literature base is comparatively thin. Existing ESG-related studies in the broader Asian and Chinese context indicate that investors do respond to voluntary corporate social performance and environmental attributes \citekey{Wang2017}, and sustainability-oriented behavior is shaped by broader strategic and social factors \citekey{Yang2022}. However, these studies do not solve the measurement problem of ESG disclosure quality itself, nor do they address Chinese-language annual and sustainability reports in a Taiwan-listed sample. That leaves an important empirical gap: we know ESG disclosure matters, and we know text analytics and LLMs can extract richer semantic information, but we lack evidence on whether LLMs can reliably outperform traditional scoring methods in assessing the quality and completeness of Taiwan firms’ ESG narratives. Your study is well positioned to fill this gap by combining expert validation, multilingual document analysis, and firm-level explanation of disclosure variation.

## Differentiation Statement

Unlike previous work that relies on keyword counts or unvalidated automated proxies to measure ESG disclosure, our study assesses disclosure quality using LLM-based semantic evaluation and validates it against expert human judgments while applying the method to Chinese/bilingual reports of Taiwan-listed firms.

## Contribution Echo

Yes — the three Phase 1 contributions are supported by the literature:

1. **Technical contribution supported:** The need for context-aware, digitally enabled assessment methods is consistent with digital innovation research \citekey{Berger2021}, and LLM/NLP work supports fine-grained document understanding with careful evaluation \citekey{Tong2022}, \citekey{Bender2021}.  
2. **Empirical contribution supported:** Prior sustainability and ESG studies show that firm capabilities and voluntary CSR/CSP vary meaningfully across firms and affect external responses \citekey{Gelhard2016}, \citekey{Wang2017}.  
3. **Practical contribution supported:** The literature on digital capabilities and sustainability performance implies value for scalable tools that improve transparency and decision-making for investors and regulators \citekey{Berger2021}, \citekey{Yang2022}.
