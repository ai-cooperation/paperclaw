# Research Positioning

# Literature Landscape

ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) disclosure assessment has gained substantial interest globally as corporations and stakeholders acknowledge the critical importance of sustainability and social responsibility in business operations. Most extant research focuses on Western markets, developing frameworks, indices, and evaluation methodologies for ESG disclosures primarily issued in English and shaped by Western regulatory environments. While this body of work offers a rich set of tools and insights, it inadequately addresses the nuances of Asian markets, particularly Taiwan, where linguistic diversity, regulatory peculiarities, and cultural factors influence both ESG disclosures and their interpretation.

Recent advances in Artificial Intelligence (AI), especially Large Language Models (LLMs), bring promising directions for the analysis of large volumes of unstructured textual data such as ESG disclosures. LLMs have demonstrated impressive capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, enhancing automation, accuracy, and contextual comprehension in various domains including financial document analysis. However, their application in ESG disclosure assessment is nascent, with a predominant concentration on English language resources and Western contexts (Bender et al., 2021). The complex linguistic features and mixed Mandarin-English usage in Taiwanese corporate reports pose a formidable challenge for standard NLP models, necessitating tailored adaptations or fine-tuning for effective ESG-related insights extraction.

Traditional ESG assessment methodologies are heavily reliant on manual reviews or rule-based systems that focus on structured quantitative data, broadly limiting their scalability and potentially embedding human biases. As a result, qualitative disclosures, which carry essential context and nuance, remain underutilized in assessment processes. Efforts to integrate AI for automated qualitative data processing show promise but currently lack robust frameworks targeting the specific demands of ESG data, such as domain-specific lexicons and multilingual contexts (Garcia-Diaz et al., 2020). Moreover, most existing AI-driven ESG studies concentrate on high-resource languages and markets, often overlooking low-resource or complex language settings such as Taiwan’s bilingual environment.

In sum, the literature reveals critical gaps particularly pertinent to Taiwan-listed companies: (1) insufficient research focused on Taiwan’s unique ESG ecosystem; (2) underdeveloped utilization of LLMs specifically attuned to multilingual ESG disclosure analysis; and (3) limited automation tools for handling qualitative ESG content efficiently and comprehensively. This suggests an opportunity to design and empirically validate an LLM-based ESG assessment framework that addresses these gaps, thereby advancing both methodological rigor and practical utility in a critical but underexplored market.

# Differentiation Statement

Unlike previous work that primarily applied LLMs and AI techniques in English-centric financial or health domains and relied on manual or rule-based ESG assessments with limited focus on Taiwan, our study develops a multilingual LLM-based analytic framework tailored specifically for the Taiwanese ESG disclosure context by incorporating linguistic nuances, regulatory requirements, and cultural considerations, thereby enabling more accurate, efficient, and comprehensive ESG assessments for Taiwan-listed companies.

# Contribution Echo

1. **Technical Contribution:** The development of an LLM-based analytic framework tailored for Mandarin-English ESG disclosures addresses the documented need for language- and context-specific NLP tools, consistent with findings regarding the challenges of multilingual unstructured data analysis (Garcia-Diaz et al., 2020) and the limitations of existing English-dominant LLM applications (Bender et al., 2021).

2. **Empirical Contribution:** Conducting comprehensive evaluations of Taiwan-listed companies’ ESG disclosures fills a notable void in the literature regarding ESG reporting patterns in Asian markets and contributes valuable empirical insights, aligning with recommendations for expanding ESG disclosure research beyond Western markets (Dueñas-Ocampo et al., 2023).

3. **Practical Contribution:** Delivering a scalable, automated ESG disclosure assessment tool supports stakeholders in Taiwan’s capital markets, echoing calls for AI-powered transparency enhancement in corporate sustainability monitoring (Allal-Chérif et al., 2021), thereby bridging research to practical impact.
