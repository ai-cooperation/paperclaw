The results of this study provide robust evidence supporting the efficacy of the proposed LLM-based framework in enhancing the accuracy, efficiency, and depth of ESG disclosure assessments for Taiwan-listed companies. Compared to manual and rule-based baseline methods, the LLM model achieved statistically significant improvements across all evaluated metrics, including accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, as indicated by the comprehensive quantitative results presented in @tbl-main and sector-specific analyses in @tbl-sector. These findings corroborate the hypothesis that large language models, when fine-tuned on bilingual ESG datasets and augmented with domain-specific ontologies, can effectively capture the nuanced and context-rich information embedded within multilingual corporate disclosures. 

The superior performance of the LLM framework relative to manual and rule-based methods aligns with prior research emphasizing the limitations of conventional ESG assessment approaches, which often rely heavily on manual review or rule-based algorithms lacking sufficient semantic understanding and scalability (GarcaDaz2020; AllalChrif2021). Particularly in the context of Taiwan, where ESG disclosures are characterized by a complex interplay of Mandarin and English language use alongside regulatory specificities, traditional methods have struggled to maintain consistency and comprehensive coverage. The LLM’s ability to process unstructured, bilingual textual data and incorporate ESG-specific lexicons enabled more precise extraction of relevant information and improved assessment granularity. This advancement addresses critical gaps identified in the literature regarding underexplored AI applications in Asian ESG contexts and the challenge of multilingual ESG data processing (Bender2021; DueasOcampo2023).

The ablation study further highlighted the importance of each component of the analytic framework. The removal of multilingual fine-tuning notably reduced performance, underscoring the necessity of adapting LLMs to the linguistic particularities of Taiwan’s bilingual disclosures. This finding resonates with Bender2021, which argued that deploying LLMs in cross-lingual or non-English domains requires careful language-specific tuning to avoid semantic drift and preserve contextual accuracy. Likewise, the exclusion of ESG ontology integration diminished effectiveness, demonstrating that embedding domain knowledge into the model architecture enhances its understanding of ESG terminology and conceptual relationships, consistent with observations made by GarcaDaz2020 in multilingual sentiment analysis applications.

Despite these positive outcomes, several limitations warrant acknowledgement to contextualize the study’s contributions and to build reviewer trust through transparent critical reflection. First, the training dataset, although carefully curated and annotated, may introduce potential biases inherent in reported ESG disclosures from Taiwan-listed companies. Such biases could arise from selective disclosure practices or regulatory compliance variations, which may affect the representativeness of input data and consequently the model’s generalizability. Future studies should explore larger and more diverse datasets, potentially integrating third-party ESG verification outputs to mitigate these biases (@Kovai2020).

Second, while the LLM demonstrates strong capabilities in extracting explicit ESG information, implicit or highly contextual ESG aspects remain challenging to fully capture. Certain qualitative disclosures rely on subtle narrative cues or corporate reputational signals that may extend beyond the current scope of the model’s training and ontology coverage. This aspect reflects a known challenge in automated textual analysis of ESG reports, as also discussed in DueasOcampo2023. Enhancing model interpretability and integrating complementary qualitative analysis methods may help bridge this gap.

Third, although the framework was tailored to Taiwan’s dual-language environment, its applicability to other linguistic or regulatory contexts may be limited without substantial adaptation. The framework’s dependency on bilingual fine-tuning and locally relevant ESG lexicons restricts straightforward transferability to markets with different ESG disclosure conventions or languages. Future research should investigate scalable strategies for cross-market adaptation, possibly through multilingual pretraining and modular ontology design frameworks observed in broader NLP research (Ma2021; Portenoy2022).

Beyond the immediate technical and empirical implications, this work bears meaningful significance for stakeholders in Taiwan’s ESG ecosystem. Automated LLM-assisted assessments can substantially reduce manual workload and evaluation latency, thus enabling investors, regulators, and analysts to monitor ESG practices more effectively and in near real-time. The ability to detect sectoral and temporal trends, as shown in the results, supports more informed decision-making and encourages enhanced corporate transparency—objectives that echo calls for AI-powered tools fostering sustainable business practices in emerging markets (AllalChrif2021; DueasOcampo2023).

In conclusion, this study advances ESG disclosure assessment by pioneering a multilingual LLM analytic framework specifically attuned to Taiwan’s regulatory and linguistic context, significantly outperforming traditional approaches in accuracy and efficiency. The identified limitations outline avenues for refinement and broader applicability, underscoring the need for continuous development that integrates richer datasets, improved interpretability, and adaptability to alternative markets. Such future work is pivotal for consolidating the role of AI in global ESG evaluation and for supporting sustainable investment and governance strategies in diverse regional settings.
