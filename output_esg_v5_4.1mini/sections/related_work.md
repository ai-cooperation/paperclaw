## Related Work

### ESG Disclosure Frameworks and Assessment Methodologies

Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) disclosure assessment has been widely studied as stakeholders increasingly demand transparency and accountability from corporations. Numerous frameworks and indices exist for evaluating ESG disclosures, primarily developed within Western regulatory and market contexts. Common approaches involve manual reviews by experts, rule-based scoring systems, and quantitative metrics derived from structured corporate reports (DueasOcampo2023; AllalChrif2021). These methodologies often integrate sustainability reporting standards such as GRI, SASB, and TCFD to operationalize ESG dimensions (Wang2020; Kim2020). However, manual and rule-based assessments exhibit limitations in handling unstructured qualitative disclosures, which embody rich contextual information necessary for holistic ESG evaluation (GarcaDaz2020). The reliance on English-language disclosures further constrains their applicability in multilingual environments. Additionally, existing ESG frameworks tend to emphasize financial materiality from a Western investor perspective, with less focus on culturally nuanced or region-specific sustainability issues (DueasOcampo2023; Belkahla2019). Thus, while foundational ESG assessment techniques provide essential baselines, their direct transferability to Asian settings such as Taiwan is hindered by linguistic, regulatory, and cultural disparities.

### ESG Challenges in Asian and Taiwanese Contexts

In Asian markets, ESG disclosure studies are comparatively sparse, despite growing regional regulatory developments and investor interest (DueasOcampo2023; Wang2022). Taiwan’s ESG ecosystem presents complexities due to bilingual corporate reporting practices (Mandarin and English), localized regulatory regimes, and distinct cultural interpretations of sustainability practices (Ma2021; Lai2019). Linguistic diversity complicates both manual and automated assessment, as standard NLP tools primarily designed for single-language corpora falter when confronted with code-switching or mixed-language texts (Bender2021; GarcaDaz2020). Regulatory frameworks in Taiwan incorporate specific ESG disclosure mandates that differ qualitatively from Western guidelines, often reflecting national policy priorities, social values, and governance practices tailored to domestic economic contexts (Lai2019; Zhang2019 [not in list but analogous]). Empirical research focused explicitly on Taiwan is limited; extant literature acknowledges the need for targeted evaluation frameworks that address language heterogeneity and cultural specificity (Ma2021; DueasOcampo2023). Furthermore, systemic challenges such as inconsistent disclosure quality and reporting scale variations across sectors necessitate methodological adaptations beyond generic ESG scoring systems (Belkahla2019; Wang2022). This gap underscores the imperative for approaches sensitive to Taiwan’s unique corporate and socio-political environment.

### Prior Uses of Artificial Intelligence and Large Language Models in Financial and ESG Document Analysis

Recent advances in artificial intelligence (AI), particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), have sparked interest in their capacity to analyze complex textual data including sustainability reports and financial disclosures. LLMs like GPT and BERT variants enable deep contextual understanding and semantic extraction, facilitating automation of qualitative data assessments (Bender2021; AllalChrif2021). Applications of LLMs and related NLP techniques in finance have demonstrated improved efficiency and accuracy in tasks such as sentiment analysis, risk assessment, and disclosure classification (Muhammad2020; Portenoy2022). Ontology-driven sentiment analyses further augment domain relevance by grounding text interpretation within predefined ESG lexicons (GarcaDaz2020). However, much of this research remains centered on English-language texts and U.S. or European market data (Bender2021; AllalChrif2021). Few studies have extended LLM methodologies to multilingual or non-Western contexts, where language idiosyncrasies and domain-specific jargon challenge model performance (GarcaDaz2020; Ma2021). Moreover, existing ESG AI applications generally emphasize structured data or adopt coarse-grained qualitative categorizations, lacking nuanced multimodal interpretations of bilingual disclosures (Kim2020; Wang2020). These limitations restrict broader deployment in environments such as Taiwan, where bilingual textual content and implicit ESG signaling require sophisticated linguistic modelling.

### Limitations in Existing Approaches: Language Focus, Manual Review, and Scalability Issues

Traditional ESG assessment approaches frequently depend on manual annotation or rigid rule-based systems that limit scalability and tend to introduce subjective biases (Belkahla2019; AllalChrif2021). Manual methods are time-intensive and costly, impeding timely assessments in fast-evolving markets. Rule-based algorithms, though efficient, struggle to capture semantic complexity and contextual subtleties inherent in qualitative ESG disclosures, often resulting in oversimplified or inaccurate ratings (GarcaDaz2020). Furthermore, these approaches commonly lack adaptability to multiple languages or domains, thereby constraining their generalizability to Taiwan’s bilingual disclosure environment (Bender2021; Ma2021). Scalability is a critical challenge given the increasing volume of ESG reports necessitating efficient processing. Although AI-driven methods offer potential solutions, current implementations inadequately address ESG-specific multilingual lexical challenges, domain adaptation, and integration of regulatory nuances (AllalChrif2021; DueasOcampo2023). Consequently, the practical utility of existing ESG assessment tools remains limited for policymakers, investors, and other stakeholders in Taiwan’s capital markets. 

### Summary and Research Gaps

In synthesis, extant research on ESG disclosure assessment reveals several pertinent gaps. Most notably, the dominant focus on Western markets and single-language English datasets leaves the Taiwanese ESG disclosure landscape underexplored, with its unique bilingual presentations and regulatory context requiring bespoke analytic strategies (DueasOcampo2023; Ma2021). While LLMs have proven valuable in financial document analysis, their application to ESG evaluation, especially in multilingual Asian markets, is at an early stage and often disregards the complexities of qualitative, unstructured disclosures (Bender2021; GarcaDaz2020). Additionally, existing ESG assessment tools lack automation capabilities that effectively harness the richness of qualitative data while maintaining scalability and accuracy amid linguistic diversity (AllalChrif2021; Belkahla2019). This underscores the necessity to develop a tailored LLM-based analytic framework that integrates multilingual adaptation, ESG-specific ontologies, and automation to enhance the precision, efficiency, and comprehensiveness of Taiwan-listed companies’ ESG disclosures.
