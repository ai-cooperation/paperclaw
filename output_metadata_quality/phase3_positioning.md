# Research Positioning Document — Metadata Quality Across Academic Databases

## 1. Literature Landscape

The quality of metadata in academic databases is a critical factor for effective research discovery, accurate bibliometric analyses, and overall scholarly communication. Prior work has explored elements of metadata quality, including completeness, accuracy, timeliness, and consistency, but often these are examined in isolated contexts or with ad hoc metrics. For example, Sun et al. (2020) emphasize the importance of structured and multi-modal knowledge graphs in enhancing recommender systems by leveraging various data types and external knowledge sources, but they do not explicitly address standardized metadata quality assessment across databases [Sun2020]. Similarly, bibliometric studies, such as Sandnes (2021) and Liu et al. (2020), use metadata for analyzing research trends and technology evolution but often rely on a single database's metadata, implicitly assuming its sufficiency without systematic quality evaluation [Sandnes2021; Liu2020].

The existing literature reveals significant limitations in the assessment of metadata quality. Most research has narrowly focused on individual databases or specific domains, lacking a comprehensive cross-database comparative approach. Moreover, metrics for evaluating metadata quality vary widely—ranging from completeness measures to citation accuracy—without a unified, validated framework to standardize comparisons. This inconsistency impairs the ability to generalize findings or to recommend best practices for metadata curation across platforms.

Another important gap concerns the practical implications of metadata quality variations. While some works discuss data quality challenges, few explicitly link these issues to their downstream effects, such as potential biases in bibliometric indicators or obstacles in information retrieval. For instance, although bibliometric analyses highlight disparities in research outputs and citation practices (Sandnes, 2021), they rarely consider how metadata discrepancies across databases might introduce systematic distortions. Thus, understanding how metadata quality impacts research discoverability and evaluation remains underexplored.

Addressing these gaps, the proposed study aims to deliver a rigorous, standardized framework for metadata quality assessment and apply it empirically across leading academic databases like Web of Science, Scopus, PubMed, and Google Scholar. This cross-database perspective, combined with practical recommendations, would extend beyond prior isolated analyses, offering novel insights into the critical role metadata plays in the scholarly communication ecosystem. Targeting a journal such as *Scientometrics* leverages an established community deeply engaged with bibliometrics and research data quality concerns, further situating the work within relevant scholarly conversations [Sandnes2021; Liu2020].

## 3. Differentiation Statement

Unlike previous work that typically examines metadata quality within isolated databases or contexts without standardized metrics, our study systematically develops and applies a standardized framework to quantitatively assess and compare metadata quality dimensions across multiple major academic databases, explicitly linking these variations to their effects on research discoverability and bibliometric reliability.

## 4. Contribution Echo and Literature Support

1. **Standardized Framework for Metadata Quality Metrics** — The importance of consistent measurement in metadata quality is recognized implicitly through the fragmented approaches in bibliometric and information science research [Sandnes2021; Liu2020], motivating our technical framework unification.

2. **Comprehensive Cross-Database Empirical Analysis** — Previous studies have demonstrated the value of bibliometric analyses but typically use metadata from individual databases [Sandnes2021]. Our multi-database comparative work fills this empirical gap.

3. **Actionable Recommendations for Enhancing Metadata Quality** — While extant literature extensively documents metadata challenges [Sun2020], there is limited actionable guidance. Our practical recommendations respond directly to this unmet need, benefiting researchers, librarians, and database providers.
