# Research Positioning

# Literature Landscape

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) disclosure assessment has gained growing attention in recent years as firms and investors seek reliable, transparent information to guide sustainability initiatives and investment decisions. Research to date has primarily focused on manual or semi-automated approaches to analyzing ESG reports predominantly in Western contexts, where regulatory frameworks and reporting standards such as GRI or SASB have matured (Cheng and Shiu, 2020). However, regional differences in ESG reporting practices suggest that methods validated in one context may not generalize well to emerging Asian markets like Taiwan, which has distinct regulatory requirements and disclosure customs. This gap limits the applicability and adoption of existing ESG assessment technologies and calls for a more localized approach (Cheng2020).

On the technological front, recent advances in artificial intelligence, particularly large language models (LLMs), have demonstrated remarkable natural language understanding and analysis capabilities in various domains, including finance and sustainability reporting. Yet, empirical evaluations investigating the accuracy, consistency, and reliability of LLM-assisted ESG assessment compared to conventional human analyst methods remain scarce. Existing studies tend to either focus on purely manual approaches or traditional machine learning models such as random forests (Permatasari2021), without leveraging the contextual understanding and versatility of LLMs for nuanced, sector-specific ESG language. With the rapidly advancing LLM architectures, there is an opportunity to quantitatively benchmark their performance on ESG texts specifically, which remains underexplored.

Moreover, practical challenges related to deploying AI tools like LLMs into ESG disclosure assessment workflows are starting to emerge but have not been rigorously studied. Issues such as user acceptance, integration with existing corporate governance processes, and the provision of actionable insights for sustainability officers and regulators are critical for realizing the full potential of AI in this space. While broader AI adoption literature highlights the importance of aligning technology capabilities with user needs and organizational contexts (Jeyaraj2022), concrete guidelines tailored to ESG assessment are lacking. There is a pressing need for actionable frameworks that bridge AI innovations with practical corporate sustainability applications.

In sum, the literature reveals three main gaps: limited investigation of LLMs for ESG disclosure assessment in Asian emerging markets like Taiwan; insufficient empirical benchmarking of AI-assisted versus traditional ESG evaluation methods; and a lack of studies addressing practical implementation and stakeholder engagement with AI tools for sustainability reporting. Our study proposes to fill these gaps by developing and validating an LLM-based ESG assessment framework customized to Taiwan’s ESG disclosures, empirically evaluating its performance against human analysts, and delivering user-centered guidelines to support AI integration in corporate and regulatory ESG workflows.

# Differentiation Statement

Unlike previous work that has largely focused on manual or conventional machine learning approaches for ESG disclosure analysis in Western markets, our study applies and empirically evaluates state-of-the-art large language models tailored to the Taiwan market by incorporating local language and sector-specific nuances. By directly benchmarking LLM-assisted ESG assessments against human analysts and addressing practical implementation challenges, our study bridges methodological innovation with real-world applicability in an understudied emerging Asian market.

# Contribution Echo

1. **Technical Contribution:** The development and validation of an LLM-based ESG disclosure assessment framework customized for Taiwan’s regulatory and linguistic context is supported by the AI and domain adaptation literature emphasizing the need for localized NLP models for accurate textual analysis (Permatasari2021; Jeyaraj2022).

2. **Empirical Contribution:** Empirical evaluation comparing LLM-generated assessments with conventional human analyst evaluations addresses the identified gap in benchmarking advanced AI models for ESG, as existing studies provide limited quantitative comparisons of AI versus human performance in this domain (Permatasari2021).

3. **Practical Contribution:** Delivering practitioner-focused insights and guidelines aligns with wider AI adoption studies advocating for user-centered strategies to facilitate technology acceptance and effective integration in organizational workflows (Jeyaraj2022).
