# Research Positioning

# Literature Landscape

The assessment of ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) disclosures has drawn growing academic and practical interest due to increasing regulatory pressure and investor demand for transparency (GascoHernandez2022). Most extant ESG evaluation methods rely on manual review or rule-based systems, which often result in inconsistencies, delays, and subjective bias. Prior research generally focuses on global or Western markets, and there is a noted deficiency in exploring ESG disclosure assessment approaches contextualized for Asian markets, particularly Taiwan with its distinctive regulatory frameworks and dual language (Mandarin and English) disclosures.

Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) offer promising capabilities to address these challenges. LLMs, pretrained on vast multilingual corpora, have shown potential for nuanced text comprehension and automated analysis in diverse domains (Bender2021). However, the environmental and social risks of deploying large language models also necessitate careful tailoring and validation, especially to specific regional and regulatory contexts (Bender2021). Empirical studies demonstrating the enhanced accuracy and efficiency of LLM-assisted ESG assessments compared to expert human evaluations remain sparse, especially in settings with multilingual disclosures and culturally specific norms.

Additionally, automation in open collaboration and knowledge work through bots and AI systems has yielded mixed outcomes, including unintended demotivation and changes in user participation patterns (Kang2021). These findings highlight the importance of thoughtfully integrating LLM assistance to complement rather than supplant expert judgment in ESG assessments. The efficiency benefits of AI-based automation warrant rigorous comparison to manual processes, and equity in capturing Taiwan’s unique legal and cultural nuances requires specialized model development and validation.

In summary, the literature underscores significant gaps: few studies focus on Taiwan or similar multilingual regional markets for ESG; there is limited empirical validation of LLMs enhancing ESG disclosure accuracy and consistency; and minimal discussion exists around embedding such technologies within human-AI workflows to avoid user disengagement. This research proposes to fill those gaps by building and empirically evaluating an LLM-based ESG disclosure assessment framework tailored to Taiwan-listed companies, thereby contributing technical, empirical, and practical advances relevant to academia and industry.

# Differentiation Statement

Unlike previous work that generally focuses on Western-centric ESG assessment frameworks and either manual or preliminary AI approaches without regional adaptation and empirical validation, our study develops a Taiwan-specific LLM-based ESG disclosure assessment framework by fine-tuning multilingual models on culturally and regulatorily contextualized data, and empirically comparing its accuracy, consistency, and efficiency against expert human evaluations.

# Contribution Echo

1. **Technical Contribution:**  
Development and validation of the LLM framework tailored to bilingual (Mandarin and English) ESG disclosures align with the recognized need for language-specific and regulatory-context-aware models (Bender2021, GascoHernandez2022).

2. **Empirical Contribution:**  
Providing quantitative comparisons of LLM-assisted vs. manual ESG assessment addresses the scarcity of empirical validation in large language model applications to ESG domain tasks (Kang2021, Bender2021).

3. **Practical Contribution:**  
Delivering a replicable, scalable tool to stakeholders such as regulators and investors affirms the importance of improving ESG transparency using AI as argued in the sustainability and governance literature (GascoHernandez2022, Virmani2023).

These contributions are rooted in gaps and needs well-documented in the literature, confirming their scholarly relevance and practical significance.
